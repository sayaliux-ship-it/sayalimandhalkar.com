Tenetic Story Builder

Designing an AI storytelling workflow for enterprise media sales when the client didn't know what they wanted

My Role UX Designer, AI interaction patterns & hero flow
Team 2 UX Designers, 1 Researcher, 1 Creative Director (Photon Interactive)
Timeline 12 weeks: 4 weeks Discovery, 8 weeks Implementation
Platform Web, desktop-first with tablet support

Details have been omitted or abstracted in compliance with a non-disclosure agreement. All visuals are reproduced with permission. The work shown reflects my individual contributions as UX Designer, with ownership of AI interaction patterns and Sprints 2 and 4.

Overview

When the brief is
ambiguity itself

Tenetic came to Photon Interactive with a problem and a hypothesis. The problem: their enterprise media sales customers were losing hours every week cobbling together pitch decks out of PowerPoint, Excel exports from Nielsen Scarborough, Comscore pulls, and whatever their last deck happened to look like. The hypothesis: AI could write those decks for them.

That was the entire brief.

No flows, no feature list, no clarity on what "AI could write those decks" actually meant in an interface. The client had a vision and a product team ready to build, and they needed a design partner who could turn that ambiguity into something shippable without spending six months in discovery.

This is the part of AI-for-business work that doesn't get talked about. The client is not hiding the spec from you. They don't have one. You're being hired to help them figure out what the product is by designing it.

The Problem, Stated Plainly

A morning that should
take two minutes

Media sales reps sell air time. To sell air time, they build custom pitch decks showing advertisers why their TV network reaches the right audience. A typical deck has twenty to forty slides full of demographic data, ratings, dayparts, genre breakdowns, and competitive context. Every deck is different because every advertiser is different.

Today a rep like Ali Walton, Senior Sales Rep at a CBS local station in Chicago, spends the entire morning pulling numbers out of Nielsen Scarborough, reshaping them in Excel, pasting them into PowerPoint, styling them on brand, and writing the narrative glue between the charts. She has a template. She starts from the last deck. She rebuilds half of it anyway because "no copy paste, a lot needs to be redone."

Tenetic wanted to collapse that morning into a prompt.

The design question was not "can AI do this." It was: how much of the work do we automate, where do we hand control back to the user, and what does the interface look like when the user is co-authoring a deck with a model that sometimes gets the dayparts wrong?

Part 1

Discovery

Four weeks. Two primary user interviews, secondary research on two more accounts, a competitive audit of every AI deck tool on the market, and a structured blueprint session with the Tenetic product team.

Research Foundation

Reading the field
before touching Figma

We ran a four-week Creative Discovery sprint. Two primary user interviews, secondary research on two more accounts (Disney and KTLA video sales teams), and a competitive audit of every AI deck tool currently on the market.

Primary interviews: P1 was a Chief Platform Officer at a TV research firm managing a team of data analysts. P2 was a Strategy and Development SME on the agency side, builds client-facing proposals.

We went in with open questions: what does your week actually look like, what's the worst part of deck prep, what would you never let AI touch, when do you trust the numbers and when do you double-check them.

Research synthesis: interview data to affinity mapping to insights to design considerations

The Decision Filter

Five insights. Every feature argument
ran through them.

Five insights came out of the affinity mapping. We locked these exact phrases early because they became the decision filter for every feature argument later.

1

Ease of Use

If it's harder than PowerPoint, it won't get used.

2

Quick & Efficient

The whole point is saving the morning.

3

Refine Output

Nobody ships the first draft, AI or otherwise. This insight shaped most of the AI interaction design.

4

Compelling Storytelling

Data without narrative doesn't close deals.

5

Accuracy of Data

One wrong rating number kills the pitch and the relationship.

The sharp one is number 3. Refine Output. It's the insight that shaped most of the AI interaction design. Reps do not want a button that says "generate deck." They want a collaborator that shows its work and lets them push back before, during, and after generation.

Competitive Audit

Five tools. None of them
knew what a daypart was.

Five tools in the space: Gamma, Canva, SlideSpeak, STORYD, SlidesAI. We pulled them apart feature by feature.

The gap was clear. Every tool in the market was a general-purpose AI deck builder. None of them knew what a daypart was. None of them could ingest Nielsen or Comscore data. None of them understood that a media sales deck has a specific narrative shape: who the audience is, why this network reaches them, what the media plan costs, what the outcome should be.

That gap became the wedge. Tenetic wasn't competing with Gamma on "make any deck prettier." Tenetic was competing with PowerPoint on "make this deck, the one a CBS local station rep ships on a Tuesday morning."

Competitive landscape: Gamma, Canva, SlideSpeak, STORYD, SlidesAI feature matrix

Discovery Blueprint

Locking the model before
touching a single screen

We ran a Creative Discovery blueprint session with the Tenetic product team to lock engagement model and user hierarchy before we touched Figma.

Engagement Model: Lean Forward with Micro Lean Back elements. Users are actively working (writing prompts, editing slides, refining outlines) but we build in lean-back moments during generation so the tool feels like a partner, not a task.

Decision Type: Requires Some Thought. Reps are not in autopilot. Every prompt involves judgment about audience targeting, narrative framing, and which data to lead with.

Primary User: Salesperson. Secondary: Researcher. Tertiary: Executive reviewer.

This mattered because the client had initially pitched it as a tool for researchers (the data-heavy users). We pushed back. Researchers have their own workflow and their own tools. Sales reps are the ones losing the morning to deck building. Sales reps are also the ones with the budget to adopt a new tool.

Primary Persona

Meet Ali Walton

Ali Walton. 36. Senior Sales Rep at a CBS local station in Chicago. Quota-carrying. Builds four to six custom decks a week. Trusts data but doesn't want to do the data work. Knows her audiences, knows her dayparts, has opinions about what stories close which accounts.

Ali is not afraid of AI. She's skeptical of it. She's been burned by autocomplete, by Siri, by Copilot. She will use Tenetic if it makes her faster without making her look stupid in front of a client. She will abandon it the first time it hallucinates a rating number.

Ali is the person every design decision in this product is for.

Primary persona: Ali Walton, Senior Sales Rep, CBS Chicago

Current State Journey

The handoff is where
the friction lives

Ali does not work alone. Lauren Smith is her research analyst. Lauren pulls the raw data, Ali writes the story. The current state journey mapped both lanes because the handoff between them is where most of the friction lives.

Kickoff. Research request goes to Lauren. Lauren pulls Nielsen and Comscore. Ali waits. Ali gets the data in a spreadsheet. Ali pastes half of it into a deck. Ali realizes half of it is not what she asked for. Ali goes back to Lauren. Repeat.

The Tenetic pitch was: let Ali start the deck before Lauren finishes the data pull, and let the AI fill in the blanks intelligently when the data lands.

Current state: Ali and Lauren's dual-lane workflow

Future State

Five stages. One tool.
No waiting.

Five stages. The new journey keeps Ali in one tool from discovery call to final deck. She writes a prompt, reviews optional structured inputs, watches the outline generate, edits the outline, picks a template, and ships.

The key insight: we did not remove Lauren from the workflow. We removed the waiting. Lauren still provides data expertise, but now Ali can draft in parallel and Lauren can enrich the deck once her research is done.

Future state: five-stage journey from kickoff to ship

App Definition Statement

One sentence that ended
every scope argument

We closed Discovery with a one-sentence product definition the whole team could point to when scope fights started:

A flexible, easy-to-use storytelling tool for marketing personas who need compelling media and consumer insights to drive business outcomes efficiently.

Every word in that sentence was argued over. "Flexible" because prompts can't be rigid. "Easy-to-use" because the competitor is PowerPoint. "Storytelling" because it's not a chart tool. "Media and consumer insights" because that's the moat. "Business outcomes" because Ali sells deals, not slides.

What we left Discovery with

A five-insight decision filter the client and team could use to settle feature arguments

Part 2

Implementation

Four sprints. Eight weeks. Two designers splitting the surface area.

The hand-off from Discovery to Implementation is usually where ambiguity comes back to bite you. With AI products, it bites harder. Every screen we designed had a question behind it: what does the interface do when the model is wrong, slow, or uncertain?

We answered that question the same way every time. Give the user a control point. Show the work. Let them redirect before committing.

Style Guide

Typography for enterprise.
A semantic color system for AI.

Work Sans for headings: confident, modern, reads as enterprise without being stiff. Lato for body: the reading workhorse.

Color: Light #5434A2, Dark #091A43. Gradient blues for elevated surfaces. A semantic communication palette for system states (Info, Error, Success, Warning) because AI products throw more system states at the user than a typical SaaS tool and they need to be legible at a glance.

Grid: 12-column desktop, 8-column tablet, consistent 8px base.

Style guide: Work Sans headings, Lato body, semantic brand colors, gradient blues, semantic communication palette

Sprint 1

File Management & Navigation

Building the shell Ali
comes back to daily

Ali needs to come back to decks. Media sales is iterative. A pitch evolves over three meetings with the same advertiser. She also needs to find last quarter's deck for a similar client, duplicate it, and riff.

File Dashboard. Grid view and list view with feature parity. Recent presentations surface first. Every card has a hover action menu (Rename, Duplicate, Move, Delete, Share). The list view has the same actions as a row menu. We kept the dashboard deliberately sparse because we knew Sprint 2 was going to pack a lot of signal into the creation flow, and we wanted the home screen to feel like breathing room.

Side Navigation. Collapsed and expanded states. Collapsed is default for returning users (reps know where things are). Expanded surfaces labels for new users. The collapse/expand state persists per user. Primary nav items: Home, Presentations, Templates, Account.

One decision worth calling out: we resisted adding a "Create with AI" CTA inside every empty state. The client wanted it. We argued that cluttering empty states with AI prompts trains users to think of the AI as a nag, not a collaborator. A single, prominent Create button at the top right. That's it.

Sprint 2

Create with AI

The sprint where the
AI pattern got defined

This is the sprint where the AI interaction pattern got defined. Everything downstream of Sprint 2 inherits its rules.

The client's initial direction was a single text box. Prompt in, deck out.

We said no.

A single text box works for consumer AI. It does not work when Ali's prompt is "build me a Q2 local news pitch for a Chicago auto dealer targeting women 25 to 54 during morning drive." That prompt has five embedded variables, any of which could be misinterpreted by the model, and every misinterpretation costs Ali time she doesn't have.

The pattern we shipped:

1. Conversational prompt refinement. Ali writes a prompt. The AI reads it back to her as structured variables (audience, daypart, client type, pitch goal, tone). She can confirm, correct inline, or start over. No deck is generated until she commits.

2. Storybuilder accordion inputs. Once she commits to the prompt, she lands on a second screen with six accordion sections: Customer Strategy, Media Strategy, Presentation Style, Story Themes, Data, Additional Instructions. Each is optional. Each is chunked so she can fill what she knows and skip what she doesn't. The AI uses what's filled. Missing inputs get inferred, and every inference is marked visually in the output so she can verify later.

The accordion pattern took two rounds of critique to land. First version was a long form. Reps would see it, think "this is not saving me time," and bounce. Accordion collapsed by default, progressive disclosure, optional-by-design. Completion rate in proto testing went from "they skip most of it" to "they fill three or four sections on average." Three or four sections is enough for a good draft.

The insight this sprint bakes in: prompt, then preview, then commit. Never send the user straight from a prompt to a finished artifact. Too much can go wrong silently, and once the artifact exists, users are cognitively anchored to it and less willing to redo it.

Sprint 3

Template, Generation, Editor

The most-argued screen
in the project was a loading state

Template selection. A gallery of brand-approved templates. For CBS local, that means CBS branding. Templates are not themes. Templates are layout plus brand. Ali picks once and the AI respects the template when it generates.

Generating state. The client wanted a spinner. One rotating icon, "Generating your presentation...", ten seconds, done.

We shipped a transparent loading state. It shows each phase the AI is working through (Structuring the narrative, Pulling supporting data, Drafting slides, Applying template). Each phase has a brief copy line that tells the user what's actually happening. Each phase completes in sequence.

Two reasons. One, enterprise users are more tolerant of wait times when they understand what they're waiting for. Two, if the generation fails or gets interrupted, the user knows which phase failed, and we can surface a useful error instead of "something went wrong."

Presentation Editor. Slide-by-slide editing. Left rail with slide thumbnails, main canvas, right rail with slide-level controls (regenerate this slide, change layout, update data). The editor intentionally looks familiar to anyone who's used Google Slides or Keynote because we wanted zero learning curve for the editing part. The novel interactions are all about how you got to the deck, not how you edit the deck.

Critical rule: the editor never auto-saves over AI-generated content without the user's edit gesture. If the AI regenerates a slide, the previous version is retrievable. Ali told us, in so many words, that she would not trust a tool that overwrote her edits.

Sprint 4

Slide States & Outline Review

Closing the loop on
Refine Output

This was the sprint where we closed the loop on the "refine output" insight.

Outline Review. Before the full deck generates, the AI produces an outline: slide title, one-line summary, data source tag. Ali reviews the outline, reorders slides, deletes slides that don't fit the story, regenerates individual slide descriptions, or sends the whole thing back with a new instruction.

The outline review is the second commit point in the flow. First commit was the prompt confirmation in Sprint 2. Second is here. By the time the AI generates full slides, Ali has already signed off on the shape of the deck. Generation failures now affect execution, not direction.

Slide UI states. Each slide has states for: empty, generated, edited, regenerating, data-pending (waiting on a Comscore or Nielsen pull), and error. Each state is visually distinct. Data-pending is a specific state because Ali can now start drafting before Lauren's data is back, and we need the interface to be honest about what's finished and what isn't.

Enrichment. When Lauren drops in fresh research, any slide marked data-pending can pull the new data automatically. Ali gets a notification, reviews the enriched slide, accepts or rejects the enrichment. This is the feature that closes the current-state gap between Ali and Lauren's workflow. They're now in the same tool, working in parallel, instead of waiting on each other.

Final Solution

A prompt to a deck.
Under two minutes.

A web app that turns a one-sentence prompt into an editable, on-brand, data-backed pitch deck in under two minutes, with commit points the user controls at every step.

The hero flow Ali uses today:

Home → write prompt → confirm variables → fill optional inputs → review outline → pick template → watch transparent generation → edit slides → ship.

At every step she can back up. At every step the AI shows its work. At no step does the tool assume it knows better than her.

What Didn't Ship

The cuts, with reasons

Both decisions were made with the five-insight filter on the table. The client could see exactly which insight the feature violated. That turned a design opinion into a shared scoping decision.

Client Management

The client wanted a lightweight CRM inside Tenetic: contact records, meeting notes, deal status. We argued this violated Ease of Use and pulled focus from the core deck workflow. Pushed to v2, conditional on v1 adoption data.

Loyalty & Gamification

Streaks, badges, points for using AI features. We argued that enterprise sales reps are motivated by quota, not points, and that gamification in a professional tool reads as condescending. Killed entirely.

Reflections

Designing AI for business:
five things I'd tell the next designer

Five things I'd tell the next designer who walks into an AI-for-business project where the client hasn't figured out what they want.

1

Ambiguity is the design problem.

The client is not withholding the spec. They are hiring you to produce it. Treat the vagueness as your surface area, not your blocker.

2

Build commit points into the flow.

The worst AI interfaces generate something, hand it to the user, and make the user clean it up. Good AI interfaces interrupt themselves. Prompt, confirm, preview, commit. Every commit point is a place to catch a misread before it becomes a wasted twenty minutes.

3

Show the work.

Transparent loading is not a polish feature. It's a trust feature. Users who can see what the model is doing will forgive a slower generation. Users who can't will assume the worst when something goes wrong.

4

Make room for the human.

Every AI-generated surface in Tenetic has a visible edit gesture and a way to regenerate in scope (this slide, this section, this outline). Human-in-the-loop is not a tagline. It's a design constraint on every screen.

5

Use the insights as a filter, not a decoration.

The five insights from Discovery were not a slide in a deck. They were a scoping weapon. When the client wanted to add Client Management, the insights said no. When the client wanted a single-box prompt, the insights said no. The discipline of holding the filter is what turns a research phase into a product.
